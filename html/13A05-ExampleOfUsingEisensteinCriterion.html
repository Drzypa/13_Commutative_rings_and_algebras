example of using Eisenstein criterion


For showing the irreducibility (http://planetmath.org/IrreduciblePolynomial2) of the polynomial

P⁢(x):=x5+5⁢x+11

one would need a prime number dividing its other coefficients except the first one, but there is no such prime.  However, a suitable  x:=y+a  may change the situation.  Since  the binomial coefficients of (y-1)5 except the first and the last one are divisible by 5 and 11≡1(mod5),  we try

x:=y-1.

Then

P⁢(y-1)=y5-5⁢y4+10⁢y3-10⁢y2+10⁢y+5.

Thus the prime 5 divides other coefficients except the first one and the square of 5 does not divide the constant term of this polynomial in y, whence the Eisenstein criterion says that P⁢(y-1) is irreducible (in the field ℚ of its coefficients).  Apparently, also P⁢(x) must be irreducible.

It would be easy also to see that P⁢(x) does not have rational zeroes (http://planetmath.org/RationalRootTheorem).
